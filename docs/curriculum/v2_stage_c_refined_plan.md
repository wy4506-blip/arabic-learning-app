# Stage C Lessons 9-12 Refined Plan

Stage C begins after the learner already owns a small Arabic pack, so it should feel like the next layer of confidence, not a reset into theory.
The learner is still learning real Arabic.
The difference now is that Arabic starts giving helpful clues, and the learner starts getting a feel for how to add and handle new material more intelligently.

The learner already has:

- first real-word entry into Arabic
- a small owned Arabic pack: `كتاب`, `باب`, `قلم`
- first fixed expression success: `هذا كتاب`, `هذا قلم`
- first audio-first recognition of known content
- first usable beginner pack across reading, listening, recall, and short output

The Stage C job is to:

- grow that owned pack with one or two carefully chosen real words
- show that Arabic sometimes gives helpful visible clues
- build a first guided word-study habit without turning into theory talk
- end with one tiny supported Arabic material that feels real and manageable

Stage C should feel like:

- "I can learn a new word without starting from zero."
- "Arabic sometimes gives me clues on the page."
- "I can notice one and more than one."
- "I can get through a tiny supported Arabic card."

## Lesson 9

- lesson number: `9`
- working title: `Meet بيت, Make It Stick`
- core objective: learn `بيت` as Arabic for `house` while using a quiet beginner word-study frame
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: Stage B proved that the learner can own a small Arabic pack. Lesson 9 keeps visible learner value strong by teaching one real new word, while quietly introducing a better way to make a new Arabic word stick. It should feel like forward motion in real Arabic, not like a study-skills detour.
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can recognize, hear, recall, and say `بيت`, and feels that new Arabic words can be learned in a steadier way than pure translation memorization
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner begins noticing a useful word-study bundle of form, sound, meaning, and one light category cue, without turning that bundle into a theory lesson
- target knowledge scope: known anchors `كتاب`, `باب`, and `قلم`; new target `بيت = house`; beginner pronunciation support `bayt`; one light category cue such as `singular noun` or `one-house word`
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `contrast`, `listenTap`, `comprehensionCheck`, `recallPrompt`, `speakResponse`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner identifies `بيت` from text and audio, connects it to `house`, recalls `بيت` from meaning after support is reduced, and correctly recognizes one light category cue for the word
- review seed priority: new-word recall for `بيت` first, then sound-to-form weakness for `بيت`, then weak retention of the light category cue
- next-home expectation: move to Lesson 10, where the learner discovers that Arabic words can sometimes show a helpful clue on the page

## Lesson 10

- lesson number: `10`
- working title: `Arabic Gives You a Clue: ـة`
- core objective: notice that `ـة` can act as a helpful feminine word clue in a very small set of real beginner nouns
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: after Lesson 9, the learner has seen that a word can be learned a little more intelligently. Lesson 10 extends that feeling by showing that Arabic is not completely random on the page: sometimes a word gives a useful clue. The lesson should feel like clue-finding, not rule study.
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can spot `ـة` in simple words such as `سيارة` and `كلمة` and feels that Arabic sometimes helps them notice what kind of word they are seeing
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner starts noticing that endings can support memory and category awareness, and that pattern awareness can help future learning without needing formal grammar language
- target knowledge scope: known contrast anchors `كتاب`, `باب`, `قلم`, and `بيت`; new feminine examples `سيارة = car` and `كلمة = word`; one guided cue that `ـة` often marks a feminine noun in beginner vocabulary
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `contrast`, `explanation`, `comprehensionCheck`, `listenTap`, `recallPrompt`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner identifies `سيارة` and `كلمة`, sorts target words by presence or absence of `ـة`, and recalls that the visible clue often marks a feminine noun in this controlled beginner set
- review seed priority: `ـة` clue recognition first, then confusion between the target feminine examples and known non-target nouns, then word-specific weakness for `سيارة` or `كلمة`
- next-home expectation: move to Lesson 11, where one of these same words helps reveal another useful clue: one versus more than one

## Lesson 11

- lesson number: `11`
- working title: `One Or More? Another Arabic Clue`
- core objective: notice singular-versus-plural through one tiny supported Arabic pattern set
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: Stage C should keep building clue-awareness without becoming abstract. After seeing `ـة` in Lesson 10, the learner is ready to notice that Arabic words can also change to show one versus more than one. This keeps the stage grounded in real words while making Arabic feel more understandable.
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can tell the difference between one and more than one in a tiny Arabic set such as `سيارة / سيارات` and `كلمة / كلمات`
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner becomes less likely to treat Arabic words as frozen translation labels and starts expecting form changes to carry useful meaning
- target knowledge scope: supported singular/plural pairs `سيارة / سيارات` and `كلمة / كلمات`; low-stakes quantity cues such as one-item versus many-item visuals; known Stage B pack remains available only as stable anchors, not new plural targets
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `contrast`, `listenTap`, `comprehensionCheck`, `recallPrompt`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner distinguishes singular from plural in text and audio, selects which form means more than one, and recalls at least one plural form or plural meaning after the comparison support is reduced
- review seed priority: singular-plural pair confusion first, then weak recognition of the `-ات` pattern in the controlled examples, then quantity-to-form mismatch errors
- next-home expectation: move to Lesson 12, where the learner uses the owned pack plus these new clues to handle one tiny supported Arabic material

## Lesson 12

- lesson number: `12`
- working title: `You Can Read a Tiny Arabic Card`
- core objective: process one very short fully supported Arabic card or micro-material built mostly from known content plus a small amount of glossed Stage C content
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: Stage C must end with a real handling moment, not a pattern summary. After Lessons 9-11, the learner has a pack, a better way to study one word, and two early clue-types. Lesson 12 turns those gains into one tiny supported Arabic card so the stage ends with visible value and a sense that Arabic is becoming processable in small real pieces.
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can work through a tiny supported Arabic card or micro-text, understand its main meaning, and feel that Arabic is becoming something they can handle in small real pieces
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner begins chunking known content, tolerating one or two glossed new items, and using helpful clues instead of panicking when material becomes slightly bigger than a single word or line
- target knowledge scope: mostly known material such as `هذا كتاب`, `هذا قلم`, and `بيت`; one or two lightly glossed Stage C items such as `سيارة` or `سيارات` inside a tiny supported card or structured material; explicit chunk support and meaning scaffolds
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `modeling`, `explanation`, `comprehensionCheck`, `arrangeResponse`, `recallPrompt`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner orders or matches the tiny material correctly, answers one main-meaning question, identifies one clue-bearing item inside the material, and recalls one key word or short line after guided processing
- review seed priority: text-chunk instability first, then weak retention of glossed Stage C items, then failure to use known pack items as anchors while reading the supported material
- next-home expectation: if stable, move into Stage D with the feeling that Arabic now has clues and a learnable process; if unstable, surface a short supported-card review before the first independent learning lesson
