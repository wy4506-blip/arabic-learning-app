# Stage C Lessons 9-12 Plan

Stage C begins from the accepted Stage A and Stage B foundation and does not restart from abstract theory.
The learner already has:

- first real-word entry into Arabic
- a small owned Arabic pack: `كتاب`, `باب`, `قلم`
- first fixed expression success: `هذا كتاب`, `هذا قلم`
- first audio-first recognition of known content
- first usable beginner pack across reading, listening, recall, and short output

The Stage C job is to turn that pack into early pattern awareness and guided self-learning ability.
The learner should start feeling:

- Arabic words can be studied in a repeatable way
- Arabic forms can give clues, not only meanings
- a small amount of pattern awareness makes Arabic less random
- very short Arabic material is becoming processable

## Lesson 9

- lesson number: `9`
- working title: `A Word Has Four Parts`
- core objective: study one new Arabic word through form, sound, meaning, and a simple category note
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: Stage B proved that the learner can own a small Arabic pack. Lesson 9 now turns that trust into method by showing that a new word should be learned as more than a translation label. This begins Stage C without becoming abstract because the learner still leaves with one new real word.
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can learn one new Arabic word, `بيت`, and can explain that learning a word means noticing how it looks, sounds, means, and what kind of word it is
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner begins a repeatable guided word-study routine that will later support more independent learning
- target knowledge scope: known anchors `كتاب`, `باب`, and `قلم`; new target `بيت = house`; one simple category cue such as `singular noun`
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `explanation`, `contrast`, `comprehensionCheck`, `listenTap`, `recallPrompt`, `speakResponse`
- completion evidence: the learner identifies `بيت` from text and audio, connects it to `house`, recalls `بيت` from meaning after support is reduced, and correctly recognizes one simple category note for the word
- review seed priority: new-word recall for `بيت` first, then sound-to-form weakness for `بيت`, then weak retention of the form-sound-meaning-category study frame
- next-home expectation: move to Lesson 10, where the learner starts noticing that some Arabic word forms carry useful visible signals

## Lesson 10

- lesson number: `10`
- working title: `The -ة Signal`
- core objective: notice that `ـة` is a common feminine word signal in a very small set of real beginner nouns
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: after Lesson 9, the learner is ready to see that words are not only memorized one by one. Lesson 10 gives the first low-load pattern clue: some Arabic words visibly signal a category, which makes the language feel less arbitrary without turning the lesson into grammar teaching
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can spot `ـة` in simple words such as `سيارة` and `كلمة` and feels that Arabic words can give useful clues on the page
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner starts noticing that form can signal category and that pattern awareness can support memory and future learning
- target knowledge scope: known contrast anchors `كتاب`, `باب`, `قلم`, and `بيت`; new feminine examples `سيارة = car` and `كلمة = word`; one guided cue: `ـة` often signals a feminine noun in beginner vocabulary
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `contrast`, `explanation`, `comprehensionCheck`, `listenTap`, `recallPrompt`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner identifies `سيارة` and `كلمة`, sorts target words by presence or absence of `ـة`, and recalls that the target signal often marks a feminine noun in this controlled set
- review seed priority: `ـة` signal recognition first, then confusion between target feminine examples and known non-target nouns, then word-specific recall weakness for `سيارة` or `كلمة`
- next-home expectation: move to Lesson 11, where one of these same feminine words helps reveal a first one-vs-more-than-one pattern

## Lesson 11

- lesson number: `11`
- working title: `One And More Than One`
- core objective: notice singular-versus-plural through one small supported Arabic pattern set
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: Stage C should not stop at one form clue. After seeing `ـة` in Lesson 10, the learner is ready to notice that Arabic words can also change to show one versus more than one. This adds real pattern awareness while staying concrete and beginner-safe
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can tell the difference between one and more than one in a tiny Arabic set such as `سيارة / سيارات` and `كلمة / كلمات`
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner becomes less likely to treat Arabic words as fixed translation labels and starts expecting meaningful form change across related words
- target knowledge scope: supported singular/plural pairs `سيارة / سيارات` and `كلمة / كلمات`; low-stakes quantity cues such as one-item versus many-item visuals; known Stage B pack remains available only as stable anchors, not new plural targets
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `contrast`, `listenTap`, `comprehensionCheck`, `recallPrompt`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner distinguishes singular from plural in text and audio, selects which form means more than one, and recalls at least one plural form or plural meaning after the comparison support is reduced
- review seed priority: singular-plural pair confusion first, then weak recognition of the `-ات` plural cue in the controlled examples, then quantity-to-form mismatch errors
- next-home expectation: move to Lesson 12, where the learner uses the owned pack plus these new pattern clues to process a tiny supported Arabic material

## Lesson 12

- lesson number: `12`
- working title: `Read A Tiny Supported Arabic Card`
- core objective: process one very short fully supported Arabic text or structured material built mostly from known content plus a small amount of glossed Stage C content
- why this lesson exists after Stage B: Stage C must end with a real handling moment, not only pattern explanation. After Lessons 9-11, the learner has a pack, a word-study frame, and first pattern clues. Lesson 12 turns those gains into a small readable Arabic material so Stage C ends with visible value and not a dry theory feeling
- learner-visible outcome: the learner can work through a tiny supported Arabic card or micro-text, understand its main meaning, and feel that Arabic is becoming processable in small real pieces
- hidden self-learning outcome: the learner begins chunking known content, tolerating one or two supported new items, and using pattern clues to reduce panic when material is slightly bigger than a single word or line
- target knowledge scope: mostly known material such as `هذا كتاب`, `هذا قلم`, and `بيت`; one or two lightly glossed Stage C items such as `سيارة` or `سيارات` inside a tiny supported card or structured material; explicit chunk support and meaning scaffolds
- recommended V2 item types: `goal`, `input`, `modeling`, `explanation`, `comprehensionCheck`, `arrangeResponse`, `recallPrompt`, `feedback`
- completion evidence: the learner orders or matches the tiny material correctly, answers one main-meaning question, identifies one pattern-aware item inside the material, and recalls one key word or short line after guided processing
- review seed priority: text-chunk instability first, then weak retention of glossed Stage C items, then failure to use known pack items as anchors while reading the supported material
- next-home expectation: if stable, move into Stage D with the feeling that Arabic now has patterns and a learnable process; if unstable, surface a short supported-material review before the first independent learning lesson
